# GF.sh — Skills4People / The AI Scientist
## DEMO VIDEO SCRIPT  |  2 minutes  |  Target: scientist audience

---

### [0:00 – 0:18]  THE HOOK  *(voice-over, no screen)*

> "You're a chemist. You have a hypothesis — maybe a new cryoprotectant for cell preservation, maybe a novel biosensor for inflammation markers in blood. You know exactly what you're trying to prove. You have the chemical understanding to evaluate whether a protocol makes sense.
>
> But before you touch a single pipette, you're spending days — sometimes a week — buried in supplier catalogs, safety datasheets, protocol repositories, and budget spreadsheets.
>
> That's not science. That's administration. **We built a tool to give that time back to you.**"

---

### [0:18 – 0:35]  INPUT  *(screen: hypothesis input field)*

> "Let's say you want to test whether trehalose outperforms DMSO as a cryoprotectant for HeLa cells. You type that in — in plain English, the way you'd write it in your lab notebook."

*[Type into the field]:*
> *"Replacing sucrose with trehalose as a cryoprotectant will increase post-thaw viability of HeLa cells by at least 15 percentage points compared to DMSO, due to trehalose's superior membrane stabilisation at low temperatures."*

> "That's it. That's the full input."

---

### [0:35 – 0:55]  LITERATURE QC  *(screen: QC result panel)*

> "In seconds, the system searches six academic sources simultaneously — Semantic Scholar, PubMed, arXiv, OpenAlex, and protocol repositories — and tells you: has this been done before?
>
> Here it finds two relevant papers and flags 'similar work exists'. As a scientist, you already knew trehalose is promising — but now you have the references grounded in your exact hypothesis, not a generic search result.
>
> You didn't have to open PubMed once."

---

### [1:00 – 1:30]  THE PLAN — AND THE CRITIQUE  *(screen: plan + critique panel)*

> "Now the full experiment plan generates. Protocol steps grounded in published research. Every reagent with a catalog number — Sigma-Aldrich, ATCC — and a line-item budget. A phased timeline with decision gates. A validation design with statistical analysis plan and success criteria.
>
> **But here's what makes this different.**
>
> The AI doesn't just generate the plan — it immediately critiques it. It scans its own output for six categories of weakness: missing controls, insufficient sample size, validation gaps, safety oversights, feasibility issues, statistical design problems.
>
> Right here it flags: 'No vehicle control specified for DMSO group' and 'Sample size of n=3 insufficient for the stated 15-point viability threshold — recommend n=6 minimum based on expected variance.'
>
> As a scientist, you know those are real issues. You would have caught them yourself — eventually. The AI caught them in the same breath it generated the plan."

---

### [1:30 – 1:50]  THE FEEDBACK LOOP  *(screen: correction modal + prior learnings)*

> "And here's where it gets smarter over time. You agree with one critique but disagree with another — maybe you know from your lab's experience that n=4 is sufficient for this cell line.
>
> You click 'Suggest Correction', type in your reasoning. The system stores that with a semantic embedding. The next time anyone on your team runs a similar cryo experiment, that correction is automatically injected as context.
>
> **The tool learns from your expertise. Not from the internet — from you.**"

---

### [1:50 – 2:00]  CLOSE  *(voice-over)*

> "We're researchers ourselves. We know what it costs to lose a week to planning before the real work starts. Skills4People compresses that into minutes — and gets sharper every time a scientist uses it.
>
> **GF.sh. Tools for people who actually do the science.**"
